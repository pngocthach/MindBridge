Context: STEAM for Vietnam is a non-profit running EduOne, a free online platform delivering coding and STEM courses to Vietnamese K-12 students through a network of volunteer teachers. This brief bundles two operational problems. Problem 1 - Personalization: learners currently follow one shared path regardless of starting level, learning pace, or goals, and the dropout rate exceeds 50%; volunteer teachers have to adjust content by hand for each class. Falling model costs now make real-time personalization at scale possible without adding staff — a fit for STEAM's volunteer-based model. Problem 2 - Content Creation: the content team is thin relative to demand (5 courses, 1 full-time content person + 10 volunteers, 40–50 hours to produce one complete lesson). AI-generated content can shorten authoring time while keeping human review before publishing — suited to a non-profit's resource constraints. Requirements: Build a solution that (1) recommends level-appropriate learning paths/exercises to each student in real time instead of uniform class content, and (2) speeds up new-lesson/course production via AI-assisted drafting with a human-review step before publishing. Minimum deliverables: a demoable prototype (live URL or video), a public GitHub repo, an explainable AI architecture (why this path/content was recommended), and a 1–2 page pilot roadmap with EduOne. Vietnamese is required (the learners are Vietnamese students); content must suit Vietnamese K-12 learners in natural, simple language. Pilot: 1 course, 1 class, 20 students. Judging criteria: The core rubric applies (this brief doesn't add custom weights). Measured against: real-time per-student recommendation (demoed on sample data with per-student recommendation logs) and reduced lesson-creation time via AI drafting with human review (end-to-end demo with before/after time comparison). Anti-patterns to avoid: solutions that only work on clean sample data, AI content reaching students without human review, mockup/slideshow demos with no real AI, and total dependence on expensive paid APIs that a non-profit can't scale.
